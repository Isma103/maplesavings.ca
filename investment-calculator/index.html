Investment Calculator — Frequently Asked Questions

It simulates your investment month by month, converting your chosen compounding frequency into an equivalent monthly rate, then applies growth and contributions one month at a time. This handles mismatched compounding and contribution frequencies — like quarterly compounding with monthly contributions — accurately.

End Amount projects how much your investment will be worth. Contribution solves for the periodic deposit needed to reach a goal. Return Rate solves for the annual return needed. Starting Amount solves for the lump sum needed today. Invest Length solves for how long it would take to reach your goal.

There's no single right answer — it depends on what you're invested in and your risk tolerance. This calculator doesn't recommend a rate; it's a tool to see the effect of different assumptions. Treat any rate as an estimate, not a guarantee, since real returns vary year to year and can be negative.

Compounding frequency is how often your return is calculated and added to your balance. Contribution frequency is how often you add new money. These don't have to match — many real accounts compound daily or monthly while you contribute monthly, and this calculator handles that combination correctly.

A TFSA shelters all growth and withdrawals from tax for life. An RRSP gives a tax deduction today and defers tax until withdrawal, typically at a lower rate in retirement. A taxable account has no room limit but growth is taxed annually. Most Canadians benefit from filling TFSA and RRSP room first — see exactly how much an RRSP contribution would save you with our RRSP & FHSA Calculator.

Money contributed at the beginning of a period earns a full period of growth before the next contribution, while end-of-period money doesn't start growing until the next period. Over many years, beginning-of-period contributions produce a meaningfully larger end balance.

For the Contribution, Return Rate, Starting Amount, and Invest Length modes, the calculator searches within a wide but bounded range (e.g. up to a 1000% annual return or 100 years). If your goal can't be reached even at that extreme, it tells you instead of returning a misleading number.

The compounding math is standard, well-established financial mathematics, built to handle any combination of compounding and contribution frequency correctly. That said, this tool assumes a constant rate of return for illustration purposes — real investments fluctuate. This is not investment advice.
