Tax Calculation Methodology

How MapleSavings calculates federal and provincial income tax — including data sources, the calculation approach, Quebec-specific adjustments, and known limitations.

Overview

MapleSavings calculates Canadian income tax using the same progressive bracket system used by the Canada Revenue Agency. The calculator applies federal and provincial tax brackets to taxable income (income minus the basic personal amount), then shows how RRSP and FHSA contributions reduce that taxable income and therefore reduce the total tax owing.

All calculations run in your browser using JavaScript. No data is sent to a server.

Core Calculation Engine

Step 1 — Progressive tax on full taxable income

For each of federal and provincial, tax is applied bracket-by-bracket directly to your full taxable income — gross income minus deductions like RRSP contributions, but before the basic personal amount:

function calcGrossTax(income, brackets) { const taxableIncome = Math.max(0, income); let tax = 0; for (const b of brackets) { if (taxableIncome <= b.min) break; tax += (Math.min(taxableIncome, b.max) - b.min) * b.rate; } return tax; }

Step 2 — Basic personal amount credit

The basic personal amount (BPA) is a non-refundable tax credit, not a deduction from taxable income. It's worth your BPA multiplied by the lowest bracket rate, and is subtracted from the gross tax computed in Step 1:

function calcTax(income, brackets, personalAmount) { const grossTax = calcGrossTax(income, brackets); const bpaCredit = personalAmount * brackets[0].rate; return Math.max(0, grossTax - bpaCredit); }

This matters because it's mathematically different from subtracting the BPA before applying brackets: that approach would shield income at your marginal rate instead of the lowest rate, understating tax for anyone whose income reaches above the bottom bracket. The two approaches only agree when your full taxable income stays within the bottom bracket.

Step 3 — Quebec federal abatement

Quebec residents pay a reduced federal tax because Quebec operates its own provincial tax system separately. The CRA provides a 16.5% abatement on the federal portion for Quebec residents:

if (province === 'QC') fedTax *= 0.835; // 16.5% federal abatement

Step 4 — RRSP / FHSA savings

Savings are calculated as the actual tax difference before and after the deduction — not as contribution × marginal rate. This handles bracket crossings correctly (when a deduction moves you from one bracket to another):

function calcRRSPSavings(income, rrspRoom, province, B) { const before = calcTotalTax(income, province, B); const after = calcTotalTax(Math.max(0, income - rrspRoom), province, B); return Math.max(0, before - after); }

When both RRSP and FHSA are entered, combined savings are calculated by stacking both contributions in a single tax difference call — this prevents double-counting when the two contributions together cross a bracket boundary.

Step 5 — Marginal rate display

The displayed marginal rate is the rate that applies to the next dollar of income at the current income level. For Quebec, the federal component of the marginal rate is multiplied by 0.835 to reflect the abatement.

Known Limitations

  • Ontario surtax: Ontario applies an additional surtax of 20% on Ontario tax above approximately $5,315 and 56% on Ontario tax above approximately $6,802. This surtax is not currently included in our Ontario estimates. For income above approximately $80,000 in Ontario, our estimate of provincial tax may be understated.
  • CPP and EI: CPP contributions and EI premiums are not calculated. They reduce after-tax income but do not reduce taxable income in the same way as RRSP/FHSA contributions (CPP generates a 15% federal tax credit; EI generates a 15% federal tax credit).
  • Nova Scotia BPA phase-out: Nova Scotia's basic personal amount phases out for income between approximately $25,000 and $75,000. We use the headline BPA figure, which may overstate the personal amount for some income ranges.
  • Capital gains: Not modeled. Capital gains are subject to an inclusion rate (50% in most cases) before being added to income, with different rules for gains above $250,000.
  • Dividends: Not modeled. Eligible and non-eligible dividends have gross-up and dividend tax credit mechanisms that significantly affect effective tax rates.
  • Quebec-specific deductions: QPP (instead of CPP), QPIP premiums, and Quebec-specific refundable credits are not modeled.
  • 2025 federal bottom rate: The 2025 federal budget proposed reducing the bottom federal bracket rate from 15% to 14%. We model this as a blended 14.5% rate for the full calendar year (reflecting the proposed mid-year implementation). If legislation has changed, this rate may need updating.
