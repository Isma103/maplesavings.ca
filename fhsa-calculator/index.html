FHSA Calculator — Frequently Asked Questions

The FHSA annual limit is $8,000 in your first year. If you had unused room from a prior year, up to $8,000 carries forward, allowing a maximum of $16,000 in a carry-forward year. The lifetime maximum across all years is $40,000.

FHSA contributions are deducted from your taxable income for both federal and provincial tax, the same way an RRSP contribution is. Every dollar contributed saves tax at roughly your marginal rate. Unlike an RRSP, withdrawals for a qualifying first home purchase are also completely tax-free — you get the deduction going in and no tax coming out.

If you never purchase a qualifying first home, you can transfer your FHSA balance to your RRSP or RRIF tax-free, with no effect on your RRSP contribution room, or withdraw it directly as taxable income. The account must close by December 31 of the year you turn 71, or 15 years after you first opened it — whichever comes first.

Yes. You can contribute to both an RRSP and an FHSA in the same tax year, and both reduce your federal and provincial taxable income dollar-for-dollar. For a first-time homebuyer with room in both, combining them gives you the largest combined deduction — see our RRSP & FHSA Calculator for the combined view.

The FHSA is generally more flexible. It gives you a tax deduction on contributions AND tax-free withdrawals for a first home purchase, with no repayment obligation. The Home Buyers' Plan lets you withdraw up to $60,000 from an existing RRSP tax-free, but you must repay it over 15 years. You can use both the FHSA and the HBP together on the same home purchase.

You must be a Canadian resident, at least 18 (or 19 in some provinces), and a first-time homebuyer — meaning you (or your spouse/common-law partner) have not owned a home you lived in as your principal residence at any time in the current year or the preceding four calendar years.
