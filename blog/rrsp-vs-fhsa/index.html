RRSP vs FHSA: Which Is Better for Canadians in 2025?

Both the RRSP and the FHSA let you deduct contributions from your taxable income — meaning both reduce the taxes you owe today. But after that, they work very differently. The right choice depends on whether you plan to buy a home, and how much contribution room you have in each account.

What Is an RRSP?

The Registered Retirement Savings Plan (RRSP) is Canada's main tax-sheltered retirement account. Contributions reduce your taxable income dollar-for-dollar, and the money grows tax-deferred inside the account. You pay income tax only when you withdraw.

2025 RRSP Key Facts

2025 contribution limit: $32,490 (or 18% of your 2024 earned income, whichever is lower)

2026 contribution limit: $33,810

2025 deadline: March 2, 2026 — contributions by this date count for the 2025 tax year

Unused room: Carries forward indefinitely from any year after 1990

Withdrawals: Taxed as ordinary income in the year you withdraw

The RRSP is available to anyone with earned income in Canada. It is best suited for retirement savings, though the Home Buyers' Plan allows a one-time withdrawal of up to $35,000 for a first home purchase — which must be repaid over 15 years.

What Is an FHSA?

The First Home Savings Account (FHSA) launched in 2023 specifically for first-time homebuyers. Like the RRSP, contributions are fully tax-deductible. Unlike the RRSP, qualifying withdrawals to buy a first home are completely tax-free — no repayment required.

2025 FHSA Key Facts

Annual limit: $8,000 in your first year with an FHSA account

Carry-forward: Up to $8,000 of unused room from the prior year carries forward — so a max of $16,000 in a carry-forward year

Lifetime maximum: $40,000

Qualifying withdrawals: Completely tax-free when used to buy a first home

If you never buy a home: Transfer to RRSP or RRIF tax-free, or withdraw as taxable income

Account deadline: Must close by December 31 of the year you turn 71, or 15 years after opening — whichever comes first

To open an FHSA, you must be a Canadian resident, at least 18 years old, and a first-time homebuyer (you or your spouse/common-law partner cannot have owned a home you lived in at any time in the current or past four calendar years).

Which Should You Max First?

If you are a first-time homebuyer

Max the FHSA first. Both accounts give you the same upfront tax deduction — but the FHSA withdrawal to buy your first home is completely tax-free. RRSP withdrawals are taxed. For a first-time buyer, the FHSA is the better deal on the withdrawal side. Once your annual FHSA room is used ($8,000 or up to $16,000 with carry-forward), then contribute to your RRSP for additional tax savings.

If you are not a first-time homebuyer

The FHSA is not available to you. Your main registered account for tax deductions is the RRSP, with a 2025 limit of $32,490. The TFSA is available to everyone but does not provide a tax deduction — it shelters future growth and income from tax.

Can You Use Both in the Same Year?

Yes — you can contribute to both an RRSP and an FHSA in the same tax year. Both contributions reduce your federal and provincial taxable income dollar-for-dollar. For a first-time homebuyer with room in both accounts, combining the two delivers the maximum combined tax deduction.

Example: If you contribute $8,000 to your FHSA and $18,000 to your RRSP in 2025, you reduce your taxable income by $26,000. At a combined marginal rate of 33%, that saves approximately $8,580 in taxes.
