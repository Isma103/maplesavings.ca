How to Reduce Your Income Tax in Canada (2025)

Reducing your Canadian income tax legally comes down to using registered accounts and understanding which deductions apply to your situation. The strategies below are the ones that make the biggest difference for most Canadians — all based on confirmed 2025 CRA rules.

The key principle

Every dollar you contribute to an RRSP or FHSA reduces your taxable income by one dollar. You save taxes at your combined federal + provincial marginal tax rate — typically 20%–50% depending on your income and province.

Strategy 1: RRSP Contributions

1

Registered Retirement Savings Plan (RRSP)

Who it's for: Anyone with earned income in Canada.

How it works: Contributions reduce your taxable income dollar-for-dollar. The money grows tax-deferred inside the account. You pay income tax only when you withdraw — ideally in retirement when your income and rate may be lower.

2025 contribution limit: $32,490 (or 18% of your 2024 earned income, whichever is lower). Unused room from prior years carries forward indefinitely.

2025 deadline: March 2, 2026 — contributions by this date count for your 2025 tax return.

Example: At $90,000 income in Ontario (combined marginal rate ≈ 33.89%), a $15,000 RRSP contribution saves approximately $5,084 in taxes.

Highest impact for most Canadians

The RRSP is available for as long as you have earned income and contribution room. You must convert it to a RRIF (or annuity) by December 31 of the year you turn 71.

Strategy 2: FHSA Contributions (First-Time Buyers)

2

First Home Savings Account (FHSA)

Who it's for: First-time homebuyers who are Canadian residents, at least 18 years old.

How it works: Like the RRSP, contributions are fully tax-deductible. Unlike the RRSP, qualifying withdrawals to buy a first home are completely tax-free — no repayment required. This makes the FHSA the better deal for first-time buyers.

2025 contribution limit: $8,000 in your first year with an FHSA. Up to $8,000 of unused room from the prior year carries forward, allowing up to $16,000 in a carry-forward year. Lifetime maximum: $40,000.

If you never buy a home: Transfer the balance to your RRSP or RRIF tax-free, or withdraw it as taxable income.

Best for first-time homebuyers

If you are a first-time buyer with room in both an RRSP and an FHSA, contribute to the FHSA first. Both give you the same deduction, but the FHSA withdrawal is tax-free. See the full RRSP vs FHSA comparison guide.

Strategy 3: TFSA for Tax-Free Growth

3

Tax-Free Savings Account (TFSA)

Who it's for: Any Canadian resident 18 years of age or older with a Social Insurance Number.

How it works: TFSA contributions are not deductible — they do not reduce your taxable income today. However, all growth, dividends, and capital gains inside the TFSA are permanently tax-free. Withdrawals are also completely tax-free and do not affect benefits like OAS or GIS.

2025 contribution limit: $7,000. Lifetime room (since the TFSA launched in 2009): $102,000 as of 2026 for someone who has been eligible since inception.

Best use: Investments that generate dividends or capital gains you would otherwise pay tax on annually. Also ideal for savings you may need before retirement, since TFSA withdrawals are tax-free at any time.

Strategy 4: Home Buyers' Plan (RRSP Withdrawal)

4

Home Buyers' Plan (HBP)

Who it's for: First-time homebuyers who already have money in an RRSP.

How it works: You can withdraw up to $35,000 from your RRSP tax-free to purchase or build a qualifying first home. The withdrawal is not added to your income — but you must repay the amount to your RRSP over 15 years starting the second year after the withdrawal. If you miss a repayment, that year's amount is added to your income.

Can you combine with FHSA? Yes — you can use both the FHSA and the HBP together to maximize the funds available for a first home purchase.

Strategy 5: Basic Personal Amount

5

Basic Personal Amount (BPA)

Who it's for: All Canadian taxpayers — this applies automatically.

How it works: The federal basic personal amount for 2025 is $16,129. This amount is effectively tax-free — you do not pay federal income tax on the first $16,129 of income. Each province also has its own basic personal amount that reduces provincial tax.

The BPA is not something you need to "claim" separately — it is built into how your employer withholds taxes (TD1 form) and automatically applied on your tax return.

Automatic, applies to everyone

Reducing Income Tax — Frequently Asked Questions

The fastest way is to contribute to an RRSP before the March 2, 2026 deadline. Contributions reduce your taxable income dollar-for-dollar and save you taxes at your marginal rate. First-time homebuyers should also contribute to an FHSA for additional deductions and tax-free withdrawals.

Your RRSP tax savings equal your contribution multiplied by your combined marginal tax rate. At a 30% combined rate, a $10,000 RRSP contribution saves approximately $3,000. The 2025 RRSP limit is $32,490, or 18% of 2024 earned income — whichever is lower.

A TFSA does not provide an upfront deduction — contributions don't reduce your taxable income today. However, all growth, dividends, and withdrawals inside a TFSA are permanently tax-free. It's best for investments that would otherwise generate taxable income. The 2025 limit is $7,000.

The RRSP deadline for the 2025 tax year is March 2, 2026 — 60 days after December 31, 2025. Contributions made by this date can be deducted on your 2025 tax return.
