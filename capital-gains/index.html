Capital Gains Tax Calculator — Frequently Asked Questions

Only 50% of a capital gain is taxable in Canada — this is called the inclusion rate. That taxable half is added to your other income for the year and taxed at your normal federal and provincial marginal rates. There is no separate "capital gains tax rate" — it's just regular income tax applied to half the gain.

No. The federal government proposed raising the inclusion rate from 50% to 66.7% on gains above $250,000 per year, first delaying it to January 1, 2026, then formally cancelling the increase on March 21, 2025. The inclusion rate remains 50% for all individuals. The one change that was kept is the Lifetime Capital Gains Exemption limit, raised to $1,250,000 for qualifying small business shares and farm or fishing property.

The LCGE lets individuals shelter up to $1,250,000 (2025) of capital gains from tax on the sale of qualified small business corporation shares, or qualified farm or fishing property. It does not apply to gains on stocks, ETFs, real estate other than a principal residence, or most other investments. This calculator does not model LCGE eligibility — if you're selling a qualifying business, farm, or fishing property, consult a tax professional.

Usually not. Gains on the sale of your principal residence are generally exempt from capital gains tax in Canada under the Principal Residence Exemption, provided you designate the property as your principal residence for every year you owned it. This calculator is intended for taxable capital gains such as stocks, ETFs, cryptocurrency, or investment property — not an exempt principal residence sale.

Common strategies include holding investments inside a TFSA or RRSP (where gains aren't taxed the same way), realizing capital losses in the same year to offset gains, spreading a large gain across multiple tax years if possible, and timing a sale for a lower-income year to reduce your marginal rate. An RRSP contribution can also offset the additional tax a capital gain creates — see our RRSP & FHSA Calculator.

The 50% inclusion rate is the same federally and in Quebec. What differs is the provincial tax rate applied to that taxable amount — Quebec's provincial brackets are generally higher than most other provinces, and Quebec residents receive the 16.5% federal tax abatement that applies to all their federal tax, including tax on capital gains.
